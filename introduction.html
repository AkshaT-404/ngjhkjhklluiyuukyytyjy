A Brief Introduction about

R A J A S T H A N

Steeped in monarchy to the fullest, Rajasthan is a captivating tourist destination, which is famous the world over. Visitors from across the globe go to this residence of Raja-Maharaja to witness and enjoy its rich cultural legacy. Rajasthan is rich in lively and colourful traditions, arts, heritage, and culture, and it is full with unique attractions. Its architectural wonders, including palaces, forts, havelis, monuments, caverns, tombs, mosques, and temples, are the ideal places to see its regal past. The vibrant culture of Rajasthan magnificently emerges in its fairs and festivals, which are so colourful and vivacious that even the desert of Rajasthan comes alive and appears to be an oasis of mirth during the festive season, punctuating the barrenness of this captivating state. Rajasthan is equally rich in its delectable cuisine, captivating animals, and incredible adventure, and it is proud of its illustrious heritage. RAJASTHAN is a magical place that will leave you spellbound. Rajasthan, which is a combination of the Sanskrit words "Raja" (King) and "Sthana" (Land) or the Persian word "St(h)an)" with the same meaning, literally translates as "The Land of Kings." The first mention of Rajasthan may be discovered in a stone inscription from 625 CE. The word Rajasthan first occurs in literature in the 1829 book Annals and Antiquities of Rajasthan or the Central and Western Rajpoot States of India, while Rajputana first appears in print in George Thomas' 1800 autobiography Military Memories. According to John Keay's book India: A History, the term Rajputana was first used by the British in 1829. John Briggs used the term "Rajpoot (Rajput) princes" rather than "Indian princes" while transcribing Ferishta's account of early Islamic India.
